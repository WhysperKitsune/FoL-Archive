Day 3 flavor

The new King, Jane Drayton, surveyed the remaining members of the medieval reenactment group. Earlier that day, Gilbert had volunteered to try to walk back to town and call for help. Gilbert shook his head, trying to remind himself not to think of Gilbert as if he was already dead. Yes, the walk to town was long, and he’d had few supplies, but that didn’t mean he would definitely die. Besides, his sacrifice – no, his choice to volunteer – meant that the group could still vote to banish someone else.

“Who should we choose?” asked King Jane.

“I think Peter is suspicious,” said someone else in the court. “His behavior has been changing constantly. On the first night after the first murder, he came back and started acting totally differently. Then, he did it again today! I think he’s just bad at lying, and it’s starting to show through.”

“Wait,” said Peter. “I can explain.” He pulled out a scrap of paper. “You see, I’ve been working on a calligraphy project, so I haven’t been getting very much sleep–”

“That has nothing to do with the accusations against you,” said King Jane.

The rest of the medieval reenactment group nodded in agreement, and sent Peter down the road. Yet, as he began to walk away, a piece of paper fell from his garments.

Someone pulled it out. “It’s clearly in the style of an illuminated manuscript,” they said. “But the paper it’s on is modern. I don’t think he can be one of the people we’re looking for.”

As they began to walk back to the castle, one member of the group stood back. The King approached her, frowning.

“Isabel?” she asked. “What’s wrong?”

“Nothing is wrong,” said Isabel, smiling. “I was actually here with the DEA, with three criminals to apprehend. But now that they’ve been apprehended, I can go home!”

“No you can’t!” said Jane. “Your tires were slashed too, just as surely as ours were.”

Isabel shrugged. “I have a motorcycle in the back of my van,” she said.

“Will you at least send help?” asked Jane.

“Why would I do that?” said Isabel. “That’s not part of my job.”

She climbed onto her motorcycle and drove away, leaving the rest of the medieval reenactment group bewildered.

The wind rustled through the shadowy corridors of the castle as its residents slept. Yet the castle was colder, slightly, now that eight of its residents had been claimed by death’s touch, or set out on futile attempts to return to town. As the castle’s Prince adjusted his circlet, he wondered how many more of his compatriots’ lives would be claimed before the dawn.

As he stood at the window, he felt an icy wind blow through the room, chilling him to the bone and extinguishing the small, flickering candle whose flame was the only light he had. He hastily attempted to relight it, before realizing that even without signal he could still make use of his smartphone’s flashlight.

He swung it around for a moment, then froze. In the bright light of the his smartphone’s flashlight beam, he had seen a face, hooded but unmistakably human.

The stranger scoffed. “Even here, you rely on the trappings of modernity,” they said. “How shameful.”

“Maybe I wouldn’t be using a smartphone if you weren’t trying to fucking murder us!” said the Prince. “Like, what else do you want me to do? Die?”

“It’s not too late to change, you know,” said the stranger, extending a hand.

The Prince screamed. He somehow doubted that the stranger intended to leave him alive. He hoped that someone would be able to hear him, in the castle.

Finally, he heard footsteps outside the doorway, and another scream. The door burst open, and one of the other members of the reenactment group appeared. Yet their candle shed light onto the floor outside the Prince’s door, where two new corpses were lain on the ground, covered in a tapestry.

When the Prince finally tore his eyes away from the sight and looked back at the stranger, they had vanished.
